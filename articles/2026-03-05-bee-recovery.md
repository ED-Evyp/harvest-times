After years of alarming headlines about collapsing pollinator populations, researchers across Europe are reporting cautious good news: in regions where regenerative and low-input farming practices have been adopted at scale, wild bee populations appear to be stabilising — and in some cases, recovering.

## Signs of Recovery

A five-year monitoring programme across agricultural zones in Greece, Italy, and Portugal has documented an increase in wild bee species richness in areas where farmers have reduced pesticide applications and introduced flowering field margins.

The findings, presented last month at a conference in Bologna, are preliminary but encouraging. "We're not out of the woods," said lead researcher Dr. Amalia Costa, "but the trajectory has changed in some of these landscapes."

## What Farmers Are Doing Differently

The farms showing the strongest pollinator recovery share a handful of practices:

1. **Reduced or zero neonicotinoid use** — replaced with targeted biological controls
2. **Wildflower strips along field edges** — even 1–2 metre margins make a measurable difference
3. **Later mowing schedules** — allowing ground-nesting bees to complete their lifecycle
4. **Intercropping with flowering species** — borage and phacelia are popular choices

## The Economic Case

Beyond ecology, farmers are beginning to see practical benefits. Orchards with strong local pollinator populations have reported improved fruit set without the cost of renting commercial honeybee hives — a service that has become increasingly expensive.

"It took three years to see the difference," says Giorgos Manolis, a fruit grower in Thessaly. "Now I don't hire hives for my cherry trees anymore."

The lesson, researchers say, is that ecological recovery is possible — but it requires patience, coordination across neighbouring farms, and a shift in how we think about the field edge.
