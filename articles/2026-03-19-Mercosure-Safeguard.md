After years of fraught negotiations, the EU-Mercosur trade deal is finally moving toward reality — and with it, a long-promised protective net for European agriculture. Regulation (EU) 2026/687, adopted on 11 March 2026, establishes the legal machinery to defend EU farmers if the opening of markets to South American agricultural exports goes wrong.

## Signs of Political Pressure Paying Off

A long-running campaign by European farming associations — from French cereal growers to Greek fruit producers — argued that the standard WTO safeguard process was too slow and too cumbersome to respond to real market shocks. The new regulation is a direct response to those concerns.

The Commission is now required to monitor imports of sensitive products continuously and publish a formal assessment at least every six months. If import volumes surge by more than 5% above the three-year average while simultaneously undercutting domestic prices by 5% or more, that is treated as prima facie evidence of market disruption — enough to trigger a formal investigation.

"We're not against trade," one French farm association representative said during the legislative process. "We just need to know that someone is watching."

## What the Regulation Actually Does

The farms and sectors most concerned about Mercosur competition cover a specific set of products. The regulation lists them explicitly:

1. **Beef and poultry** — both fresh and frozen, including processed preparations
2. **Dairy products** — milk powders, cheese, and infant formula
3. **Grains and sweeteners** — maize, rice, sugar for refining, and honey
4. **Other sensitive goods** — eggs, garlic, ethanol, and biodiesel

For all of these, the Commission must act quickly if trouble arrives. Investigations into sensitive products must conclude within four months. In urgent cases, provisional safeguard measures can be imposed within 21 days of opening an investigation, and if a Member State requests emergency action, Brussels has just five working days to respond.

## The Economic Case for Farmers

Beyond the legal mechanics, the regulation reflects a simple concern: that cheaper South American imports, arriving in volume, could undercut European producers before the market has time to adjust.

Definitive measures — freezing further tariff cuts or restoring duties to pre-agreement levels — can remain in force for up to four years in total. That window is designed to give the industry time to adapt, not merely to block competition indefinitely.

"It took three years to see the difference," says Giorgos Manolis, a fruit grower in Thessaly who has watched import competition reshape his sector. "You need time on your side."

The lesson, trade analysts say, is that market opening can coexist with farmer protection — but it requires clear rules, fast institutions, and a willingness to act before the damage becomes irreversible.
