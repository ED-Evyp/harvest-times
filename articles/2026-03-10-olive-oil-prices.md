Extra virgin olive oil prices have reached historic highs this spring, following two consecutive seasons of reduced yields across the Mediterranean basin. The culprit is a combination of prolonged drought, an unusually warm winter that disrupted flowering, and increasing pressure from the olive fruit fly.

## A Market Under Pressure

Wholesale prices for extra virgin olive oil have risen sharply compared to three years ago, with some premium Cretan and Kalamata varieties reaching prices that would have been unthinkable a decade ago. The ripple effect is visible on supermarket shelves across Europe.

Smaller producers, many of whom operate family groves with thin margins, are caught between rising costs and uncertain yields.

## Growers Respond with Innovation

Not all producers are suffering equally. Those who have invested in drip irrigation, modern milling equipment, and direct-to-consumer sales channels are weathering the storm better.

"We moved 40% of our sales online three years ago," says Katerina Voulgaridis, who runs a 200-tree grove in the Peloponnese. "This year, we sold out before December."

## What to Expect This Year

Agronomists are cautiously optimistic about the 2026–27 harvest. Winter rainfall across Crete and the Peloponnese was above average, and irrigation reservoirs are at healthy levels. However, full recovery of yields is unlikely before 2027.

For consumers, the advice is simple: buy good oil when you find it at a fair price, and store it properly — cool, dark, and away from heat sources.
